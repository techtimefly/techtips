Reusing one password across accounts is the single biggest everyday security
risk most people carry. When one site is breached — and sites are breached
constantly — attackers take the leaked email-and-password pairs and try them
everywhere else. That's called *credential stuffing*, and it's automated, cheap,
and effective.

A password manager fixes this properly: every account gets its own long, random
password, and you only ever remember one. Here's how to set it up so it sticks.

## Why not just use your browser?

Browser-saved passwords are better than nothing, and modern browsers have
improved a lot. But a dedicated manager still wins on the things that matter:
it works across every browser and device, it stores more than passwords
(recovery codes, cards, secure notes), and it makes auditing — finding weak and
reused passwords — a one-click job.

## Step 1 — Pick a manager

Any reputable, well-audited manager is fine. Look for three things:

- **A clear security track record** — published audits, a public history of
  how they've handled incidents.
- **Cross-platform apps** — phone, computer, and a browser extension.
- **Encrypted export** — you should always be able to get your data back out.

Avoid anything that can't explain how your data is encrypted, or that has no
apps for the devices you actually use.

## Step 2 — Create your master password

This is the one password you'll memorise, so make it strong *and* memorable. The
best approach is a **passphrase**: four or five random, unrelated words strung
together. It's long enough to resist guessing but easy for a human to recall.

> Your master password can never be recovered — that's the point. Write it down
> once, store it somewhere physically safe, and don't lose it.

Turn on two-factor authentication for the manager itself while you're here. It's
the one account that protects all the others.

## Step 3 — Import and audit

Most managers can import passwords your browser already saved. Do that first —
it gives you the full picture in one place.

Then run the manager's built-in security check. It will flag:

1. **Reused passwords** — the same password on multiple sites.
2. **Weak passwords** — short or guessable ones.
3. **Compromised passwords** — ones already seen in known data breaches.

Don't try to fix everything at once. You'll burn out.

## Step 4 — Replace passwords gradually

Work through the flagged list in priority order:

- **First:** email, banking, and the password manager. If someone controls your
  email, they can reset everything else.
- **Next:** shopping, social, and anything with a saved card.
- **Eventually:** the long tail of accounts you barely use.

For each one, open the site's *change password* page, let the manager generate a
fresh random password, and save it. A few a day and you'll be done within a
couple of weeks.

## What good looks like

When you're set up, logging in is *faster* than before — the manager fills
credentials automatically — and a breach on any single site no longer threatens
the rest of your accounts. That's the whole goal: contain the damage so one bad
day stays one bad day.
