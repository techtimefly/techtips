Everyone knows they should back up their data. Almost nobody does it well. The
problem usually isn't laziness — it's that "back up your files" is vague advice.
The **3-2-1 rule** turns it into something concrete you can actually check.

## The rule

Keep:

- **3** copies of your data
- on **2** different types of storage
- with **1** copy kept off-site

That's it. The number of each isn't magic — it's just enough redundancy that no
single failure wipes you out.

## Why each number matters

**Three copies** means your live data plus two backups. Two copies sounds fine
until you realise a backup can fail silently — and you only discover it the day
you need it. A third copy is your margin.

**Two types of storage** protects against a whole *class* of failure. If both
backups sit on identical external drives from the same batch, one firmware bug
or power surge can take both. Mix it up: an external drive and a cloud service,
say.

**One copy off-site** is the one people skip, and it's the one that matters
most. A fire, a flood, or a burglary doesn't care how many drives you owned if
they were all in the same drawer.

> Ransomware changed this maths. Modern attacks deliberately encrypt connected
> backups too. An off-site copy that isn't permanently plugged in is now a
> defence, not just disaster insurance.

## A setup that actually works

Here's a concrete version most people can run without thinking about it:

1. **Copy 1 — your device.** The working files on your laptop or phone.
2. **Copy 2 — a local backup.** An external drive with automatic backup
   software, or your operating system's built-in tool. Automatic is the key
   word: a backup you have to remember to run is a backup that won't happen.
3. **Copy 3 — the cloud.** A reputable backup or sync service. This is your
   off-site copy, and it updates itself.

Two types of storage (local drive + cloud), one off-site (the cloud), three
copies total. The rule is satisfied — and after the initial setup, none of it
needs your attention.

## Test it — a backup you've never restored isn't a backup

This is the step everyone skips. Once a setup is running, **actually restore a
file** from each backup. Pick a document, delete your working copy, and bring it
back from the local drive. Then do the same from the cloud.

You're checking two things: that the backup contains what you think it does, and
that you remember *how* to recover. The middle of a real data-loss panic is the
worst possible time to learn either.

Put a reminder in your calendar to repeat that test a couple of times a year.
Storage degrades, software changes, and accounts expire. Five minutes of
checking now is worth far more than a backup that turns out to be empty.

## The takeaway

You don't need expensive hardware or technical skill — you need redundancy and a
habit. Set up 3-2-1 once, automate every part you can, test it occasionally, and
data loss stops being a catastrophe and becomes a minor annoyance.
